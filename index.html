Research

Partial privatization and quorum sensing evolution

For a long time, bacteria were thought not to interact socially. This is no longer the case. Research revealed that bacteria cooperate by producing costly molecules that benefit producers and even nonproducers. In some cases, such cooperative behavior is regulated cell-cell communication, quorum sensing. As both the signal molecules (autoinducers) and the molecules that it regulates are costly, how can producing individuals be ubiquitous if there are those individuals that do not produce these costly goods and yet benefit from them? This question carries real-life implications, as some of the molecules regulated by quorum sensing increase virulence and as an increase in the frequency of producing-cooperative strains are associated with higher virulence. Past mathematical models explaining how cooperation can be maintained in the population assume that molecules are fully secreted. The problem is that this is not always the case and that the partial secretion of molecules has shown to foster producing strains over non-producing ones. In this project, I investigated how partial privatization might direct selection to foster producing strains over non-producing strains. My results (forthcoming) indicate potential risks in assuming full secretion of molecules and consider when partial secretion might be can and cannot be neglected.
